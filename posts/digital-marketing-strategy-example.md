## What is a useful digital marketing strategy example?

A useful digital marketing strategy example shows how a brand earns attention without becoming dependent on noise. Hermès is a good case because the lesson is not “be luxury.” The lesson is restraint.

A Medium article on Hermès’ digital marketing makes the surface case: social storytelling, technology partnerships, and younger-audience relevance. That is a decent starting point. But the deeper lesson is more useful for a service business.

Hermès does not appear to treat digital as a separate personality. Digital extends the same operating system: craft, scarcity, direct control, and patient trust. Its [2024 Universal Registration Document](https://hermes.fr.digital-report.net/en/report/3625) describes the strategy around creation, craftsmanship, and an “exclusive, balanced distribution network.” It also says the network will keep strengthening omnichannel service, digitised uses, and online product offering.

That is the part most businesses miss.

They add channels before they clarify the system. They post more before they know what they are proving. They buy traffic before they can answer a lead in time. Then they call the problem “marketing.” It is usually an operations problem wearing a marketing costume.

For BBH’s audience — service businesses, founders, and operators — Hermès is useful because it shows the opposite pattern: make the brand clear first, then let the channels carry that clarity.

## What should a digital marketing strategy include?

A digital marketing strategy should include seven things: positioning, demand, proof, distribution, conversion, follow-up, and measurement. If one is missing, the system leaks.

Here is the operator’s version.

1. **Positioning:** What do you want to be known for, and what will you refuse to be known for?
2. **Demand:** What are people already searching, asking, or trying to solve?
3. **Proof:** What evidence makes you believable before a sales call?
4. **Distribution:** Which channels can carry that proof consistently?
5. **Conversion:** What is the next step when someone is ready?
6. **Follow-up:** How fast and reliably does the business respond?
7. **Measurement:** Which numbers prove revenue movement, not vanity?

Hermès is not a clean one-to-one model for a local HVAC company, agency, or B2B service firm. The economics are different. The audience is different. The level of brand equity is different.

But the structure travels.

Hermès protects the story of craft. It controls distribution. It uses collaborations selectively. It expands digital service without turning the brand into a discount funnel. That is the point: digital marketing works best when it amplifies a disciplined business, not when it tries to compensate for an unclear one.

## How does Hermès use digital without weakening the brand?

Hermès uses digital as an extension of trust, not as a replacement for trust. Its public materials keep returning to the same ideas: quality, creativity, savoir-faire, loyal clients, and a controlled distribution network.

That consistency matters.

In 2015, [Apple and Hermès announced Apple Watch Hermès](https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2015/09/09Apple-and-Herm-s-Unveil-the-Apple-Watch-Herm-s-Collection/). Apple described it as a collaboration merging Apple’s product innovation with Hermès’ heritage, iconography, and craftsmanship. Hermès’ artistic director framed it as a contemporary and functional object for everyday life.

That is a precise partnership. It did not say, “We need to look modern.” It took a modern object and wrapped it in existing brand logic.

That is the marketing lesson. Good digital strategy does not ask, “What platform is hot?” It asks, “Where can our existing advantage travel without becoming diluted?”

For a service business, the equivalent might be:

- a calculator that helps prospects estimate missed lead value;
- a case-study library built around real operational problems;
- an automated intake flow that gets a qualified lead to a human quickly;
- a useful email sequence that teaches buyers how to evaluate the service;
- a search page that answers the exact question a buyer asks before calling.

None of this is glamorous. That is why it works.

## What can service businesses learn from Hermès?

Service businesses can learn three things from Hermès: protect the core, use technology selectively, and make the customer journey feel intentional.

First, protect the core.

Most small businesses do not have a brand problem because their logo is weak. They have a brand problem because the market cannot tell what they are best at. The message shifts by platform. The website says one thing. The sales call says another. The follow-up email says nothing at all.

Hermès gives the opposite lesson. The channel changes, but the center holds.

Second, use technology selectively.

Technology should remove friction or increase trust. It should not create busywork. If an AI agent, CRM workflow, chatbot, or ad campaign does not improve speed, clarity, or revenue attribution, it is not leverage. It is decoration.

That is why BBH treats agentic systems and marketing as connected work. If your paid search campaign brings in leads but your team answers them six hours later, the campaign did its job and the business still lost. If your website ranks but the offer is vague, traffic only exposes the gap faster.

Third, make the journey intentional.

Hermès’ 2024 reporting mentions omnichannel offering, digitisation of uses, service development, and online product expansion. The language is dry. The operating idea is strong: the customer should experience one house, not a pile of disconnected channels.

A service business needs the same discipline at a smaller scale.

A buyer should be able to search, understand, trust, request, and receive a response without wondering whether the company is awake. This is where systems matter. Not because systems are impressive. Because systems protect the promise.

## How do you apply this digital marketing strategy example?

Apply the Hermès lesson by building a smaller, stricter marketing system. Do not copy luxury aesthetics. Copy the discipline.

Start with this sequence.

**1. Define the promise in one sentence.**  
If a prospect cannot repeat what you do and why it matters, your marketing will become expensive. A clear promise is not a slogan. It is a filter for every page, ad, post, and automation.

**2. Build around high-intent demand.**  
Do not start with content you feel like making. Start with buyer questions. “Emergency HVAC repair near me,” “best CRM for small agency,” and “how much should managed AI agents cost” are different levels of intent. Treat them differently.

**3. Turn proof into assets.**  
Every completed job, saved hour, shortened response time, or recovered lead can become proof. Build case studies, before-and-after workflows, screenshots, short Loom walkthroughs, and simple explainers. Proof beats polish.

**4. Connect visibility to response speed.**  
Marketing does not end at the form fill. For many service businesses, the money is in the first five minutes after interest appears. Route leads. Notify the right person. Draft the first reply. Track whether it happened.

**5. Measure what matters.**  
Rankings, impressions, and clicks are useful only if they connect to pipeline. Track calls, forms, qualified leads, booked appointments, close rates, and time to first response. Vanity metrics are allowed in the dashboard only if they serve revenue.

**6. Write down what you will not do.**  
This is the ignored part of strategy. Hermès does not behave like a volume retailer online. A service business needs similar constraints: no trend-chasing that confuses the offer, no automation that lies about being human, no ad spend without follow-up, no content that cannot point to a buyer problem.

Restraint is not passivity. It is focus.

## What is the honest catch?

The honest catch is that Hermès can afford patience because it has decades of brand equity. Most businesses cannot simply “be more exclusive” and expect demand to appear.

That is where the lesson needs translation.

A local or B2B service business should not imitate scarcity for its own sake. If you make the buying process harder without having trust, you create friction, not prestige. If you post cinematic brand films before you answer basic search intent, you are skipping steps.

The useful version is this: be disciplined about what your digital channels are for.

Some channels create discovery. Some build trust. Some capture high-intent buyers. Some improve retention. Some are just noise. The job is to know which is which before you spend.

For most service businesses, the first serious move is not a grand campaign. It is a clean operating loop:

- a clear offer page;
- local or niche search visibility;
- proof that the work is real;
- fast lead routing;
- a follow-up system that does not depend on memory;
- monthly review tied to revenue.

That is less exciting than “mastering digital.” It is also more likely to work.

## What should you do this week?

Do a one-hour audit of your marketing system.

Open your website, Google Business Profile, ads, CRM, inbox, and last five sales calls. Ask five questions:

1. Can a stranger tell what we do in ten seconds?
2. Are we visible for the searches that signal buying intent?
3. Do we show proof, or only claims?
4. How long does it take to respond to a real inquiry?
5. Which metric tells us whether revenue improved?

If the answers are weak, do not add another platform yet. Fix the loop.

Hermès is useful here because it reminds us that digital marketing is not the art of being everywhere. It is the discipline of making the right promise visible, believable, and operationally true.

For a service business, that is the work: get found, earn trust, respond quickly, and build systems that keep the promise when the owner is not watching.

That is how digital becomes leverage.

Not noise.
